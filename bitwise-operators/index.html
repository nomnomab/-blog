General Information

Before we get into anything hairy, let's define a bit:

A bit (short for "binary digit") is the smallest unit of measurement used to quantify computer data. It contains a single binary value of 0 or 1. (TechTerms)

Ok, great, but why is this so useful? Well, anything that exists on your computer is made up of these small bits.

Just take a look at some of the basic data types of C#:

The way you can determine what bit value represents a number is through a method I call bit sliding. Let's look at what I mean:

Basically a bit will go through this process:

  1. The bit moves one spot to the left
  2. It will check the right slots for any zeroes
  3. If a zero is found, repeat steps one and two
  4. Once there are no more zeroes, the bit furthest to the left will advance one spot to the left
  5. The bits to the right of it will zero out
  6. Repeat the previous steps until the desired number is reached

Now, let's play around with some bits now that you have a general idea of what one is.

Bit Operations

When dealing with bits you have various operators. These operators manipulate bits by moving them around, or flipping their values. Let's see what they look like.

OR

The OR operator is a simple one to understand. All it does is that it copies bits that exist in either operand.

Let's look at an example: 0001 OR 1000 = 1001 (1 OR 8 = 9). As you can see, the bits from both operands get copied into the output.

AND

The AND operator is another simple one. The way it works is that if a bit is shared between the operands, then it gets copied.

For example: 0101 AND 0110 = 0100 (5 AND 6 = 4).

XOR

The XOR operator basically checks for bits that are unique to each bitset.

For example: 1001 XOR 0110 = 1111 (9 XOR 6 = 15).

NAND

The NAND operator checks for shared bits from both operands and these bits equal zero.

For example: 1010 NAND 1110 = 0101 (10 NAND 14 = 5).

NOR

The NOR operator is similar to NAND in the sense that is also checks for shared bits. When shared bits are found, it returns the inverse value.

For example: 1010 NOR 1110 = 0001 (10 NOR 14 = 1).

NOT

The NOT operator just flips every bit.

For example: NOT 1111 = 0000 (NOT 15 = 0).

Left Shift

The Left Shift operator shifts every bit to the left by the amount of the right operand.

For example: 0011 << 1 = 0110 (3 << 1 = 6).

Right Shift

The Right Shift operator does the opposite of the Left Shift. It shifts every bit to the right by the amount of the right operand.

For example: 0011 >> 1 = 0001 (3 >> 1 = 1).
